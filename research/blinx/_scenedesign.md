# _scenedesign.md — Scene Design: every aspect, applied to Blinx

The culmination document. All 45 aspects from _aspects.md, applied at the
SCENE level — how whole spaces are composed, staged, lit, dressed, ended.
Object-level construction lives in _objects.md; style systems in _style.md.
Each entry ends with its LAW. Evidence: 96 frames, 331 crops, w1–w8 files.

---

# A. THE ELEMENTS AT SCENE SCALE

## A1 — LINE
Blinx scenes are built from BROKEN lines. There is no long straight contour
anywhere: rooflines sag, walls bow, canals bend, paths curve and kink. Lines
also LEAD: the pale coping edge of a canal is a drawn line that pulls the
eye toward the next pool of light; the wear-path through floor tiles is a
line drawn by feet. Vertical lines are the rarest — and when one appears (an
ice pillar, a tower) it stands like an exclamation mark because everything
around it slumps. Seams between facets act as engraved contour lines that
describe form without outline rendering.
**LAW: every long line either sags, bends, or leads somewhere. Verticals are
spent like exclamation marks.**

## A2 — SHAPE
Scenes are layered flat shapes before they are 3D spaces: the dark
foreground blob, the mid-value building mass, the pale sky gradient, the
flat moon disc. Read any frame as a collage of 4–6 flat silhouettes and it
still composes — that is the test Blinx passes. The flat graphic layer
(pennants, glyphs, crystals, sign cards) sits ON the dimensional scene as
pure unshaded shape, giving the eye instant islands of simple form amid the
sculpted detail.
**LAW: a scene must still compose when flattened into 4–6 silhouettes.
Sprinkle pure flat shapes on top for the eye to rest on.**

## A3 — FORM
Every mass is a small number of big, flat-valued facets — carved, not
modeled. Forms read at three distances: silhouette from across the scene,
facet structure at mid-range, painted surface up close. Nothing relies on
texture to explain its form; the planes do that, which is why the worlds
read solid even at 640×360. Bulges and swells (the fountain's belly, the
curtain's exhale, the pillar's waist) keep forms organic inside the faceting.
**LAW: form is explained by planes at every distance; texture is never asked
to carry structure.**

## A4 — SPACE
Space is constructed as a stack of occlusion cards: near-sharp silhouette →
mid painted structures → far soft giants → flat sky. The camera constantly
brushes past foreground masses, so the player feels INSIDE the volume. Open
areas are ringed, not endless: every plaza is hemmed by walls, every canal
by banks — space is always a room of some size, never a plain.
**LAW: build space as nested rooms with occluding layers; never give the
player an unobstructed infinite.**

## A5 — TEXTURE
Scene texture is scenic paint: dabs, washes, blotches, crackle — applied at
hand-tool scale. Each world owns a small texture vocabulary (Time Square:
stone stack + mottle + glow cards; Caves: crackle + drift + smear; Temple:
pillow tiles + relief glyphs) and repeats it religiously. Texture density
falls with distance — near ground is dab-rich, far walls go flat — so the
eye always knows where it stands in the gradient.
**LAW: few texture recipes per world, hand-scale marks, density that fades
with distance.**

## A6 — VALUE
Every scene has a value script: one darkest material (the night mottle, the
mine murk), one dominant mid (the world's body), and sparse hot spots
(windows, pools, flames, crystals) that never exceed a small fraction of the
frame. The eye is dragged hotspot → hotspot through the mid. Value also
zones the scene: playable path = lit, boundary = dark. Notan test: reduce
any Blinx frame to 3 values and the path, the goal, and the mood survive.
**LAW: one dark, one mid, few sparks. If the 3-value version loses the path,
the scene is broken.**

## A7 — COLOR
Each world is a 3–4 hue chord at high saturation, each hue spanning its full
value range. Warmth marks life and safety (windows, lamps, flowers);
cold/dark marks boundary and mystery. The complement accent (teal on
crimson, cyan in murk) is spent only on things that matter — emblems,
crystals, interactables — so the accent hue becomes a navigation channel.
Sky hue is painted into objects so the air participates everywhere.
**LAW: a world is a chord, not a palette. Warm = life, accent = important,
sky = in everything.**

# B. THE PRINCIPLES AT SCENE SCALE

## B1 — BALANCE
Scenes balance asymmetrically: a heavy dark mass on one side answered by a
bright small accent on the other — the tower against the lamp, the moon
against the roofline. Symmetry is reserved for icons (the icing crest, the
fountain's octagon), which makes symmetric things read as OFFICIAL. Overall
weight sits low: ground and mass anchor the bottom, sky and glow float the
top.
**LAW: balance mass with light, not mass with mass. Symmetry means
"official"; asymmetry means "alive."**

## B2 — CONTRAST
Every contrast type is working at once: value (flame vs murk), hue (teal vs
crimson), size (giant moon vs small roofline), shape (flat pennant vs
sculpted stone), texture (smooth ice pillar vs dabbed ivy), temperature
(amber pool vs blue night), era (neon tag on medieval brick). Scenes are
composed so that at least two contrast systems cross at the focal point —
the fountain is palest rim + center placement + pickup glow all at once.
**LAW: stack at least two contrast systems on the focal point; spread the
rest evenly so no region is contrast-dead.**

## B3 — EMPHASIS
The focal order is staged with three tools, in this priority: light first
(the brightest pool or glow wins the eye), then occlusion (the thing
cutting into the moon), then hue (the one complement accent). Sub-focal
points are chained: each pool contains a small bright thing that hands your
eye to the next pool. Nothing competes with the staged winner — rivals get
dimmed, blurred, or pushed to the frame edge.
**LAW: light decides first, occlusion second, hue third. One winner per
view; everything else is a hand-off.**

## B4 — UNITY
One material logic per world: if the world is stone + glow, nothing plastic
appears. One facet language, one texture scale, one light direction, one
chord. Even the HUD speaks the world's graphic language (rings echo the
glyphs). Unity is what lets the anachronisms (neon, pennants) read as
invited guests instead of errors — the host's rules are so clear that the
contrast is legible.
**LAW: one world = one set of construction rules. Break them only on
purpose, in flat graphics.**

## B5 — VARIETY
Within the unity, nothing repeats exactly: bricks jitter in size and hue,
tiles drift in value, flowers lean at different angles, windows cluster
without grids. Variety lives in the SMALL parameters (tilt, value, spacing)
while the LARGE recipe stays fixed — so scenes feel handmade without
feeling chaotic. New one-off props appear at regular intervals (a statue, a
planter, a relief) so every few steps has a small event.
**LAW: fix the recipe, vary the parameters; give every few steps one
one-off.**

## B6 — RHYTHM
Scene rhythm is a beat of dressed and empty: wall – wall – window cluster –
wall – door – wall. Rivets march, stripes repeat, coping slabs tick along
the canal edge — small regular beats that make the big irregular gestures
(leans, sags) legible by contrast. Vertical rhythm alternates: mass /
opening / mass / glow. The beat is slow and walking-paced; nothing stutters.
**LAW: keep a small regular beat under the big irregular gestures — the
beat is what makes the gestures read as gestures.**

## B7 — MOVEMENT
The eye enters at the brightest pool, rides the coping line or wear-path,
bounces hotspot to hotspot, and rests on the one far soft giant. Meanwhile
the world itself moves in loops: pickups spin at walking tempo, flames
flicker, sparkles drift, banners sway — always 2–4 moving things per view,
never zero, never twenty. Stillness is the world's default; motion is the
dressing that proves it's alive.
**LAW: stage the eye path like a relay race; keep 2–4 gentle loops moving
per view, no more.**

## B8 — PROPORTION / SCALE
Scale is played in octaves: the huge (moon, towers, statue), the human
(doors, lamps, planters), and the tiny (rivets, flowers, sparkles) — and the
octaves are stacked in single views so the body can measure itself against
all three at once. Deliberate wrongness is the charm: the moon too big, the
door slightly small, the teacup lantern head — a confection logic where
appetite, not engineering, sets sizes.
**LAW: compose every view with huge + human + tiny together; let appetite
bend scale where realism would flatten it.**

## B9 — PATTERN
Patterns are the world's dialects: brick courses, tile pillows, coping
slabs, rivet rows, candy stripes. Each is a simple repeat made alive by
per-unit jitter (size, value, rotation) and interrupted by age (moss takes
whole tiles, stains streak bricks). Patterns cover the BIG quiet surfaces —
walls, floors — so the eye gets order there and spends its attention on the
one-off props.
**LAW: pattern the big quiet surfaces, jitter every unit, let age interrupt
the grid in whole units.**

## B10 — REPETITION
Assets repeat; instances never match. The same lamp appears twenty times in
Time Square but each leans differently, each halo falls on different stone,
each pool overlaps different props. Repetition is economical AND thematic —
a factory-built world where time made every copy age differently. The
player never sees the seam because the VARIANT is what the eye reads, not
the asset.
**LAW: repeat assets freely; never repeat instances. Context — lean, light,
wear, neighbors — is the disguise.**

# C. LIGHT & AIR

## C1 — LIGHT DIRECTION
One sun (or one moon) per world, and every painted mark agrees with it:
rivet hot-dabs all upper-left, snow caps all on up-facing planes, moss all
on sky-facing surfaces, brick bevels all pale-top/dark-bottom. The
consistency is what makes PAINTED light read as REAL light — the player
never catches two sources arguing. Night worlds cheat the same way: the
"moon direction" governs the big planes while practicals govern the small
pools, and the two systems never overlap confusingly.
**LAW: one dominant direction governs all painted detail; practicals own
only their pools. Never let two directions argue on the same surface.**

## C2 — PRACTICALS & POOLS
Light sources are OBJECTS (lamps, braziers, windows, crystals), and every
one lands on the ground as a pool — a painted ellipse 4–5× the fixture's
size. The pools chain into paths; the sources cluster where the designer
wants you (doors, plazas, pickups). Fixtures are small and humble; their
pools and halos are huge and generous — the light is bigger than the lamp,
which is exactly how it feels to walk at night.
**LAW: every practical casts a pool, pools chain into paths, and the light
is always bigger than the fixture.**

## C3 — BLOOM & EMISSIVE
Emissives are pure saturated color with a bloom that swallows the fixture —
the lamp halo eats its own stem, the crystal's glow is bigger than the
crystal, neon glyphs bleed past their edges. Bloom size encodes brightness:
the hotter the source, the larger the feather. Nothing emissive is ever
rendered crisp — crispness is reserved for solid matter; light is always
allowed to overflow its container.
**LAW: light overflows its container. Crisp = matter, feathered = light;
bloom size = temperature of the source.**

## C4 — SHADOW GRAMMAR
Three shadow types, each with a job and a shape: CAST shadows are big soft
patches painted across walls and ground, ignoring texture grids (the shade
of unseen things); CONTACT shadows are small dark dabs with crisp cores
gluing every prop to the ground; RECESS shadows are dark dabs deep in folds,
joints, and under overhangs — painted darker than physics would. Nothing
floats, because everything sits in its dab; nothing is flat, because every
fold owns its dab.
**LAW: cast = soft patches that ignore grids; contact = small dabs under
everything; recess = darker than physics. All three, always.**

## C5 — ATMOSPHERE
The air is dressed: snow sparkle in Everwinter, dust and embers in the
Forge, bloom-haze everywhere at night. Particles are few (dozens), small,
slow, looping, and distributed through the full depth of the view — near,
mid, far — so the volume between you and the wall has a body. Each world's
air has its own particle and its own tint (pink-lavender in Everwinter,
blue-brown in Time Square, teal-dark in the Mine).
**LAW: every world owns one particle and one air tint; spread the drifters
through all depths; dozens, not hundreds.**

## C6 — DISTANCE TREATMENT
Distance is rendered as softness and paleness, not fog walls: the far
giant (moon, mountain, tower) is deliberately lower-detail and blurrier
than the near roofline cutting it; distant walls flatten into their chord's
pale value; texture density thins to nothing. Sharpness is a budget spent
ONLY on the near layer. The far always keeps one big readable silhouette —
distance simplifies, it never dissolves.
**LAW: far = soft, pale, simple, but with one strong silhouette. Spend
sharpness only where the player stands.**

# D. STAGE CONSTRUCTION

## D1 — COMPOSITION
Views are composed like dioramas: a low horizon so the sky and its giant
disc perform; the path entering at a frame edge and bending (never
straight to vanishing point — a bend promises more); one dominant mass
off-center; one bright accent answering it on the other side; and a dark
foreground mass clipping at least one frame edge. Thirds are used loosely —
the focal point sits near a third but the composition's real skeleton is
the VALUE arrangement: dark frame, lit heart, pale far layer.
**LAW: dark frame, lit heart, pale far — the value skeleton first; thirds
and mass placement second; the path always enters bending.**

## D2 — DEPTH LAYERING
Four layers minimum in every composed view: (1) near silhouette, dark and
sharp, often brushing the camera; (2) mid ground where the play happens,
fully painted; (3) the far soft giant; (4) the flat sky gradient. Layers
are separated by value steps, texture density, and sharpness — never by fog
color alone. Between layers: occlusion. Something near always cuts
something far.
**LAW: four layers, separated by value + density + sharpness, glued by
occlusion. No layer ever reads at the same cost as another.**

## D3 — PERSPECTIVE & CAMERA
The camera sits slightly above and behind, looking down a gentle slope of
ground — enough to read the floor's wear-path and pools (the floor is a
map) but not so high the walls lose their height. Verticals stay near-
vertical (no dramatic convergence); the world reads as a model box viewed
at doll-house distance — which is exactly the maquette fantasy. Foreground
props pass close to the lens constantly, confirming you are INSIDE the box.
**LAW: camera = doll-house height: enough down-angle to read the floor as a
map, low enough to keep walls tall. Feed the lens a foreground prop often.**

## D4 — NEGATIVE SPACE
Empty fields are confident: the crimson candy wall is one huge flat
nothing; the night sky is a bare gradient; the dark between pools is
unpainted mottle. Negative space is where the eye RESTS and where the
accents detonate — the teal crest works because the crimson field around
it is empty. Blinx never fills a surface just because it's big; big
surfaces get ONE ornament, centered, like a crest.
**LAW: big surfaces stay empty; one centered ornament per field. The
emptiness is what the ornament is FOR.**

## D5 — THRESHOLDS
Transitions are physical and celebrated: archways, gates, bridges, curtain
doors. A threshold is always dressed — an arch gets icicles, a gate gets
stripes, a bridge gets coping — because crossing is an event. Thresholds
also frame the NEXT scene like a picture-in-picture: you see the next
world's chord and light through the opening before you enter it, a promise
that pulls you forward.
**LAW: every transition is a dressed, framed opening that previews the next
space's light and color. Crossing must feel like an event.**

## D6 — BOUNDARIES
Scenes end decisively and thematically: canals vanish into dark arched
mouths, paths pinch into gates, plazas are hemmed by leaning walls, the
cave closes with its own rock. The boundary is always ARCHITECTURE or
DARKNESS — never an invisible wall, never a raw edge. Boundary treatment is
the world's own material, just dimmed: the player reads "the world
continues but not for you" instead of "the level ends."
**LAW: end every scene with the world's own architecture or its own
darkness. The edge says 'goes on, not for you' — never 'content ends.'**

## D7 — HORIZON & SKY
The sky is a flat vertical gradient — deep at top, warmer or paler at the
horizon — and it is the biggest single color field in any vista, so it sets
the chord's key. One giant soft disc (moon) or giant silhouette lives in
it, ALWAYS cut by the roofline layer. Skyline silhouettes are irregular and
storybook: towers, gables, crenellations — a jagged hem between the world
and the sky.
**LAW: sky = one gradient + one giant + one jagged skyline hem. The giant
is always occluded; the hem is never a straight line.**

# E. TIME & LIFE

## E1 — WEAR & PATINA
Every surface in every scene carries time: stain streaks under rims, moss
on sky-facing planes, speckle on old stone, wear-polish on the walking
line, rust seams in the Forge, soot in the Mine. Wear is applied as ORGANIC
BLOTCH that ignores the man-made grid beneath it — ivy eats brick courses,
stains streak across tiles. Wear is also ZONED: the path is the most worn,
the edges the most mossed, the high places the most weathered — time has a
map and it matches how the space is used.
**LAW: age every surface with grid-disobeying blotches, and zone the wear
by use: paths polished, edges mossed, tops weathered.**

## E2 — GROWTH & DECAY
The organic and the built are in constant negotiation: ivy masses climb
walls, moss recolors tiles, flowers are PLANTED (tended) while ivy SPREADS
(untended). Scenes show both directions — the gardener's row and the wild
tide-line — because the tension between tended and untended is what reads
as "lived in for centuries." Growth always starts from somewhere legible:
a seam, a sill, a base.
**LAW: show both tended and untended nature in every scene; growth always
has a visible source.**

## E3 — HABITATION
No scene is empty of its people: lit windows say someone's home, planters
say someone gardens, a bench says someone sits, sign cards say someone
sells, laundry and awnings say someone keeps house. Habitation props are
MAINTAINED props — the planter is stained but the flowers are alive; the
wall is old but the neon is lit. The world is old but not abandoned; that
specific balance is the warmth.
**LAW: every scene needs evidence of current residents — something lit,
something tended, something for sale. Old, never abandoned.**

## E4 — MOTION & LOOPS
Per scene, 2–4 gentle loops: pickups spin and bob at walking tempo, flames
flicker in blob clusters, banners sway, sparkles drift, water scrolls.
Nothing darts, nothing buzzes — the motion tempo is calm and hypnotic,
matched to exploration. Motion marks INTERACTIVE things (spin = pickup,
glow-pulse = mechanism) so the life-layer doubles as the UI.
**LAW: few slow loops, matched tempo; motion is assigned to meaning —
if it moves, it matters.**

## E5 — GESTURE
The whole scene is posed like a single character: buildings lean together
in agreement, lamps bow over paths, trees curl, even the ground smears lean
one way. One scene = one dominant gesture family (Time Square slumps,
Everwinter stands at stiff attention in its stripes, the Caves drip and
settle). The shared gesture is what makes a world feel like ONE personality
rather than a set of props.
**LAW: one gesture family per world; every object performs it; the smears
on the ground agree with the lean of the towers.**

# F. MEANING & GAME LANGUAGE

## F1 — THEME & MOTIF
TIME is visible everywhere: clock faces, crescent crystals (time shards),
hourglass forms, ruins, patina, frozen things (birds in light, ice). Motifs
recur at every scale — the crescent is a pickup AND a window shape AND a
sign. The theme is never narrated; it is built. A player who reads nothing
still walks through a meditation on age, maintenance, and moments.
**LAW: pick the theme, then echo its motifs at every scale — pickup,
window, sign, ruin. Build the thesis; never narrate it.**

## F2 — CULTURAL ANCHORS
Each world anchors to a readable real place — canals = Venice, temple =
Mesoamerica, Time Square = medieval European market town — then bends the
anchor: Venice gets neon, the temple gets pillow tiles and green grades,
the market town gets a giant moon and glowing windows. The anchor gives
instant legibility; the bend gives identity. The anchor is in the
SILHOUETTE and the MATERIALS, not in scholarly detail.
**LAW: anchor each world to one readable place, then bend it with the
game's own language. Anchor in silhouette, bend in dressing.**

## F3 — ANACHRONISM
The collisions are deliberate: neon glyph tags on medieval brick, a factory
graphic language (pennants, rings, HUD) laid over hand-built worlds,
steampunk plates in a forge. Anachronism is the fiction made visible — the
Time Factory is literally intruding on old worlds. The rule is that the
intrusions are FLAT, SATURATED, and GLOWING while the host world is
sculpted, chord-bound, and worn.
**LAW: intrusions are flat, saturated, glowing; the host is sculpted,
chorded, worn. The collision is the story — keep both sides pure.**

## F4 — TYPOGRAPHY & SIGNAGE
Text is painted on warped cards and awnings — chunky, hand-drawn, slightly
irregular letters that belong to the world's material (painted wood, neon,
icing). Signs hang where shops are; numbers and symbols mark gates and
machines. Type is DRESSING first, information second — you feel the
marketplace before you read it. No type ever floats un-anchored: every
letter is ON something.
**LAW: type is painted on things, in the world's material, chunky and
hand-made. Felt first, read second.**

## F5 — SYMMETRY & HERALDRY
Symmetry is rare and therefore loaded: the icing crest, the fountain's
octagon, gate emblems, the moon's disc. Symmetric = official, sacred,
branded — the mark of an institution (the Time Factory, the town, the
holiday). Everything alive and organic is asymmetric. The two grammars
never mix: a crest is never crooked, a cottage is never mirrored.
**LAW: symmetry belongs to institutions; asymmetry belongs to life. Never
let the two grammars blur.**

## F6 — NARRATIVE PROPS
Props imply events: the fractured statue base (something broke, long ago),
the worn path (many feet), the pickups in the fountain bowl (someone left
offerings?), the barred grate, the abandoned mine cart. Each prop answers
"what happened here?" without a word. The best props are DUAL-USE — story
plus function: the statue's fractured base is also the anti-float skirt;
the wear-path is also the guide.
**LAW: every hero prop implies an event; prefer props that tell the story
AND do a job.**

## F7 — TONE & REGISTER
Cozy × eerie, always in pairs: tulips under a too-big moon, neon tags in
the medieval alley, candy stripes watched by a dark tower. The comfort sits
in light pools; the wrongness looms in dark mottle behind it. Register is
storybook-at-midnight: safe enough to explore, strange enough to wonder.
The tension is never resolved — that unresolved hum is the world's
personality.
**LAW: pair every comfort with a wrongness; comfort in light, wrongness in
dark; never resolve the tension.**

## F8 — PATH READABILITY
The path is written four redundant ways at once: light pools chain along
it, the wear-pattern in the floor polishes it, the coping and edges outline
it, and pickups breadcrumb it. You can follow any single channel, but all
four agree — so guidance is felt, never signposted. Dead ends are dim;
through-routes are lit. The player is led by appetite (toward warmth and
sparkle), not by arrows.
**LAW: write the path in four channels — pools, wear, edges, breadcrumbs —
all agreeing. Dim the wrong ways; never arrow the right one.**

## F9 — INTERACTIVE LANGUAGE
Interactive things speak in the flat, saturated, glowing register: crystals
are cyan crescents that spin, switches pulse, pickups bob. Static world =
sculpted and worn; interactive world = flat and lit. The channels never
blur, so the player reads affordance instantly without outlines or markers.
Rewards are staged: pickups sit IN things (fountain bowls, alcoves) so
collecting feels like being offered something.
**LAW: interactive = flat, saturated, moving, glowing; world = sculpted,
chorded, still. Never blur the channels; stage rewards inside furniture.**

## F10 — DETAIL DENSITY
Detail is spent in bands: HIGH frequency at hand level and on the path
(brick bevels, flowers, rivets, dabs), MID frequency on masses and facades
(windows, awnings, reliefs), LOW frequency on big fields and far layers
(empty crimson wall, flat sky, soft moon). Every view contains all three
bands, roughly in thirds — the eye always has somewhere busy to feed and
somewhere quiet to rest. Nothing is uniformly detailed; uniform detail is
the same as no detail.
**LAW: spend detail in three bands — busy at hand level, moderate on
masses, empty on big fields — and keep all three in every view.**

## Closing note

The 45 aspects are one machine: the elements supply the atoms, the
principles arrange them, light and air animate them, the stage frames them,
time stains them, and meaning signs them. Blinx's whole art direction is
the DISCIPLINE of these aspects agreeing with each other — one chord, one
sun, one gesture family, one theme — while every individual instance is
allowed to be a little crooked, a little worn, a little different. That is
the entire trick: **rigid laws, loose instances.**
